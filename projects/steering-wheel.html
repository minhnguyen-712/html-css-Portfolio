Steering Wheel Redesign

Ergonomic Design CAD Modeling Fabrication

On this project, I contributed to the ergonomic redesign of the steering wheel. The goal was not just to create a part, but to engineer a critical driver interface that was comfortable, safe, and reliable under racing conditions. This required a deep dive into human-centered design, force analysis, and rules compliance.

Ergonomic & Positional Design (The "Driver Fit")

Before finalizing the grip design, the team performed a digital ergonomic study using a CAD driver model. This allowed us to validate the steering wheel's position for driver comfort and control.

  • Driver Reach: We analyzed the distance from the driver's chest to the wheel, confirming an optimal range of 14-18 inches. The screenshot shows a validated distance of 0.356m (14 inches).
  • Arm Angle: This position ensures the driver's arms are never locked straight, which is critical for reducing fatigue and allowing for quick, responsive steering.
  • Steering Angle: The entire assembly was tilted at an ideal 10Â° from vertical to align with the driver's shoulders, making rotation feel natural.

Structural & Force Analysis (The "Strength Test")

A comfortable grip is useless if it breaks. I analyzed the forces the steering wheel must withstand to ensure the new design and materials (like TPU-95A) were strong enough. We used 5th percentile male strength data as our baseline:

  • Maximum Lateral Force (Turning): This is the force the driver applies to turn the wheel. Using the data, this was calculated as 196 N (98 N from each arm).
  • Maximum Axial Force (Push/Pull): This simulates a driver pulling on or pushing against the wheel. This force was calculated to be 498 N (249 N from each arm).

My material analysis confirmed that the new grip design would reliably handle these peak forces without failing.

Rules Compliance (The "Constraint")

Finally, the entire design was constrained by the official FSAE rules. Our ergonomic position had to fit within the mandated safety box. Specifically, rule T.1.4.4 requires that the top of the steering wheel must always be below the top-most point of the Front Hoop. Our design successfully met this rule, fitting within all geometric and safety constraints.
